Daily Examen: What Changes With Practice

2024-12-28 • Practice

Home | Forum | Forum Feed

The examen is a short daily prayer that traces the day with God and makes gratitude and learning concrete. Five simple movements carry it: gratitude, light, review, response, and hope. With steady practice, the examen changes how we notice, choose, and repair. These pages describe the steps, name common obstacles, and suggest small ways to keep the practice alive.

The Five Movements

  1. Gratitude: Name one gift from the day and rest in it.
  2. Light: Ask for clarity and honesty.
  3. Review: Walk through the day. Notice consolation and desolation. Look for patterns.
  4. Response: Speak truthfully about one fault and one grace. Ask for help.
  5. Hope: Look to tomorrow and choose one faithful step.

What Changes Over Time

  • Attention: Gratitude becomes quicker and more concrete. Small gifts become visible.
  • Honesty: Confession grows simpler and more direct. Drama gives way to truth.
  • Freedom: Choices move from impulse to intention. Patterns shift toward life.
  • Repair: Apologies come sooner. Plans include small amends that fit the day.
  • Hope: Tomorrow feels less vague. One step stands out and asks for action.

Common Obstacles

  • Fatigue: The mind wanders. Keep the examen short and keep the time consistent.
  • Perfectionism: The prayer stalls. Accept a simple review and return tomorrow.
  • Shame: The heart closes. Speak gently to yourself and ask for mercy.
  • Hurry: The review becomes thin. Set a two-minute timer for each movement.
  • Forgetting: Use a small card or a phone reminder until the habit holds.

Small Experiments

  • Write one sentence for each movement. Keep a single page for a week.
  • Pair the examen with a short walk. Speak the steps aloud.
  • Share one learning with a friend at the end of the week.
  • Pick one relationship and track how the examen changes your approach.
  • Once a month, read your notes and mark one theme to carry forward.

Why This Matters

The examen ties prayer to ordinary days. Gratitude becomes a habit and repair becomes practical. Over time the practice strengthens attention and hope. It gives a way to welcome both joy and struggle and to move with steadier love.
