Teaching of the Church of Unity

Yes, we condemn corrupt actions. Manipulation, financial coercion, spiritual abuse, and false teaching violate justice and mercy. In Unity, authority is for service, not domination. Giving must be free, transparent, and accountable. Guidance on sin comes from God’s revealed moral law received in Scripture and living tradition, tested by reason and a well-formed conscience, not by the whim of a leader. When harm occurs, repentance and repair are required. Crimes should be reported to civil authorities. The vulnerable must be protected.

Catholic and wider Christian perspectives

  • Catholic sources teach that scandal and simony are grave sins, stewardship of church goods requires diligence, and the faithful have a right and duty to make their concerns known to pastors. See the Catechism on simony, the Code of Canon Law on temporal goods and on the laity’s responsibility, and many recent papal warnings against clericalism. Scripture warns leaders to shepherd willingly and not for shameful gain, “not domineering” (1 Peter 5:2–3).
  • The Reformers condemned abuses tied to money and fear. Their protest pressed the whole church to purify preaching, discipline, and the practice of indulgences. Whatever one’s tradition, their critique highlights the need for transparent teaching anchored in Scripture and genuine repentance.
  • Many evangelicals and other Protestants emphasize accountability structures such as elder boards, audits, and published budgets, and they reject prosperity preaching that treats wealth as proof of favor, citing warnings like 1 Timothy 6:5–10 and Jesus’ tests of true and false teachers in Matthew 7:15–20.

Philosophical lens

Distinguish authority from authoritarianism. Legitimate authority serves the common good and invites free assent. Authoritarian control bypasses conscience through fear or flattery. Also distinguish moral formation from manipulation. Formation appeals to truth and gives reasons. Manipulation hides information and pressures compliance. Finally, separate judging acts from condemning persons. Acts can be clearly wrong while persons remain capable of repentance and change.

A practice for today

Read Ezekiel 34, Matthew 23, and 1 Peter 5:1–4. Then make a short examen:

  • Where have I seen spiritual authority used as service, and where as control?
  • Am I giving money freely, with clarity on how it is used?
  • What one concrete step can I take to support transparency and protect the vulnerable?

Act on one step this week. For example, ask your community for a published budget and independent safeguarding policy, support a trustworthy charity, or, if you know of abuse or a crime, report it to the proper authorities. If you carry wounds from spiritual abuse, seek help from trusted counselors or professionals.

Invitation

If you want, tell me what you have seen and where you feel stuck. We can name the harms clearly, look at what the tradition actually teaches about sin and stewardship, and map practical steps toward a healthier community.
