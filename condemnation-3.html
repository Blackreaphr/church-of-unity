What we condemn

  • Spiritual blackmail, fear based fundraising, and any threat that equates donations with salvation or hell.
  • Manipulation that hides budgets, punishes questions, or uses titles to silence dissent.
  • Simony and profiteering from sacred things.

What we affirm

  • God’s grace is not for sale. Giving is a free act of love for the common good.
  • Conscience must be formed by Scripture, living tradition, and honest reason, never by coercion.
  • Leaders are servants. Authority exists to teach clearly, safeguard the vulnerable, and steward resources with transparency.

Our promises

  • Clear teaching with sources.
  • A published budget and independent oversight.
  • Multiple ways to contribute: time, skills, prayer, money, or not at all. No penalties.
  • Safe channels to report abuse and, where crimes are alleged, cooperation with civil authorities.

Your choices

  • Participate with no fee.
  • Give as you freely decide.
  • Ask questions at any time and receive straightforward answers.
  • Leave or pause involvement without shaming.

Catholic and wider Christian perspectives

  • Scripture rejects domineering leadership and greedy gain: “not for shameful profit, not domineering” (1 Peter 5:2–3). Paul says, “We are not peddlers of God’s word” (2 Corinthians 2:17) and “Each one must give as they have decided in their heart, not reluctantly or under compulsion” (2 Corinthians 9:7). Jesus denounces leaders who burden others and love power (Matthew 23).
  • Catholic teaching condemns simony and clerical abuse. Many scholars note that while the Church encourages generous support of ministry and the poor, offerings must be free and accountable. The faithful have a right to make concerns known and to expect stewardship.
  • Many Protestant churches emphasize freewill offerings, audited finances, and elder oversight. Eastern Christian writers speak of synergy, the cooperation of grace and freedom, which presumes that authentic giving cannot be forced. Across traditions, threats about hell for not donating are rejected as false teaching.

A philosophical lens

Distinguish coercion from consent. Coercion uses threatened harm to override the will. Consent requires understanding, alternatives, and the freedom to say no. Also distinguish authority from authoritarianism. Authority serves shared goods and welcomes scrutiny. Authoritarianism demands submission and punishes dissent. Teaching that ties God’s favor to payment is a form of spiritual blackmail and fails these tests.

One practice for today

Freedom to Give: In the Church of Unity, your dignity is not measured by your donation. We will never say or imply that God’s love or your salvation depends on payment. We publish our budget, invite questions, and welcome gifts of time, talent, prayer, or finance, freely chosen. If you prefer not to give, you remain fully welcome. If you see misuse of funds or spiritual pressure, tell us through our safeguarding channel. We are accountable to God, to one another, and to the truth.
